## Inscribed World Heritage Sites (6) — Fuller Descriptions

- **Archaeological Ruins at Moenjodaro** — Sindh, on the Indus River. One of the best-preserved urban settlements of the Indus Valley (Harappan) Civilization, dating to roughly the third millennium BCE, notable for its planned grid layout, drainage system, and the "Great Bath." Inscribed 1980. Generally recognized under cultural criteria related to representing a significant stage in human history and bearing exceptional testimony to a civilization (broadly corresponding to UNESCO cultural criteria (ii) and (iii)-type justifications). (verify exact criteria codes)
- **Buddhist Ruins of Takht-i-Bahi and Neighbouring City Remains at Sahr-i-Bahlol** — Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, near Mardan. A well-preserved Buddhist monastic complex from roughly the 1st century CE onward, an important Gandhara-era site illustrating early Buddhist monastic architecture; Sahr-i-Bahlol is a smaller associated mound/urban remnant nearby. Inscribed 1980. Generally justified under cultural criteria concerning artistic/architectural significance (broadly criterion (iv)-type reasoning). (verify exact criteria codes)
- **Taxila** — Punjab, near Islamabad/Rawalpindi. Not a single site but a serial grouping of multiple archaeological locations (including Sirkap, Sirsukh, Dharmarajika stupa, and various monastic sites) spanning several successive ancient settlements and Gandharan Buddhist civilization, reflecting layers of Achaemenid, Greek, Scythian, Parthian, and Kushan influence. Inscribed 1980. Recognized for its testimony to the cultural exchange and development of Gandharan civilization (broadly criterion (iii)/(vi)-type reasoning). (verify exact criteria codes)
- **Historical Monuments at Makli, Thatta** — Sindh. One of the largest necropolises in the world, containing several hundred thousand tombs and monumental funerary architecture built over roughly 400+ years by rulers, saints, and scholars of Sindh (Samma, Arghun, Tarkhan, and Mughal-era patrons), noted for its distinctive brick-and-glazed-tile funerary architecture. Inscribed 1981. Recognized broadly under cultural criteria relating to artistic achievement and testimony to a cultural tradition (criterion (iii)-type reasoning). (verify exact criteria codes)
- **Fort and Shalamar Gardens in Lahore** — Punjab. A single combined inscription covering two related Mughal-era properties: Lahore Fort (a walled citadel developed substantially under Akbar and later Mughal emperors) and the Shalamar Gardens (a Mughal terraced garden complex built under Shah Jahan). Inscribed 1981. Recognized for outstanding examples of Mughal architectural and garden design (broadly criteria (i)/(ii)/(iii)-type reasoning). (verify exact criteria codes) Important: this is one inscription, not two — do not double-count.
- **Rohtas Fort** — Punjab, near Jhelum. A massive, well-preserved example of early Muslim military architecture in South Asia, built under Sher Shah Suri in the mid-16th century to help suppress local resistance and secure the Grand Trunk Road route; notable for its scale, gates, and bastions blending indigenous and Central/West Asian military architectural traditions. Inscribed 1997. Recognized broadly under cultural criteria relating to architectural/technological significance (criterion (iv)-type reasoning). (verify exact criteria codes)

Note: exact UNESCO criteria codes (i)–(x) are stated here only in general/approximate terms; confirm the precise codes against UNESCO's official site if a citation requires exactness.

## Tentative List — Real, Named Historical Entries

Pakistan's UNESCO Tentative List has, at various points, included the following real submissions. Tentative List status changes over time (entries can be added, and in principle could be inscribed or removed/lapse), so treat every entry below as needing a live-site check before being presented as Pakistan's *current* Tentative List — this is a list of names known to have appeared, not a guaranteed current snapshot:

- **Central Karakoram National Park** — a large protected natural area in Gilgit-Baltistan encompassing high-altitude peaks (including K2) and glacial landscapes; submitted as a natural-heritage candidate. (verify current status)
- **Rani Kot Fort ("Ranikot Fort")** — a large historic fort in Sindh, sometimes described as one of the largest forts in the world by perimeter; a long-standing Tentative List entry. (verify current status)
- **Chaukhandi Tombs** — a necropolis near Karachi, Sindh, known for elaborately carved sandstone tombs with distinctive geometric and figurative relief work, historically associated with the Jokhio and Baloch tribes. (verify current status)
- **Shah Jahan Mosque, Thatta** — a 17th-century Mughal-era mosque in Thatta, Sindh, known for its extensive glazed-tile decoration and unusual acoustic design; distinct from the Makli necropolis inscription though located in the same city. (verify current status)
- **Ziarat Residency** — a wooden residency building in Ziarat, Balochistan, historically significant as the location where Muhammad Ali Jinnah spent his final days; note that the original structure was damaged/destroyed by arson in 2013 and subsequently reconstructed, which may affect its heritage standing. (verify current status)
- **Nagarparkar (cultural landscape)** — a cultural/religious landscape in the Tharparkar region of Sindh near the Rann of Kutch, notable for Jain temple remains (e.g., around Bhodesar) and associated desert/hill landscape. (verify current status)
- **Fort Munro** — a hill-station/fort area in Balochistan; included here with lower confidence than the entries above. (verify — less certain this is a current or historical Tentative List entry as opposed to a general tourism site)

Given the fluidity of tentative-list status, always direct a user who needs a definitive current list to `https://whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/state=pk` rather than relying solely on the names above.

## Extraction Notes

- Keep "inscribed" (official World Heritage List) and "Tentative List" (nominated, not yet inscribed) strictly separate in any answer; never present a Tentative List name as an inscribed site.
- Lahore Fort and Shalamar Gardens count as one inscription — verify total counts sum to 6 inscribed sites, not 7.
- When a user asks for a specific inscription criterion code or exact date with citation-level precision, verify against UNESCO's live record rather than relying on this snapshot's approximate descriptions.
- Tentative List entries named above are real submissions known to have appeared on Pakistan's list, but current status (still tentative vs. later inscribed vs. lapsed) should be confirmed live, not assumed.
