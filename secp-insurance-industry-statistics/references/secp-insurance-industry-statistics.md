# SECP Insurance Industry Statistics Reference

## Regulator and Legal Framework

The Securities and Exchange Commission of Pakistan (SECP), through its Insurance Division, regulates the insurance and takaful sector in Pakistan. The core statute is the **Insurance Ordinance, 2000**, which governs licensing, solvency, and conduct requirements for insurers operating in Pakistan. Takaful (Sharia-compliant/Islamic insurance) operators are regulated under subsequent takaful-specific rules issued under SECP's rule-making powers — these are commonly referred to as the **Takaful Rules** (first issued in the mid-2000s and subsequently revised; treat the exact current rule name/year, e.g. "Takaful Rules, 2012" versus any later amendment, as something to confirm against SECP's current regulatory framework page rather than assume, since takaful rules have been revised more than once). Do not cite a specific Takaful Rules year with confidence unless it has been verified against SECP's current publications.

## Market Structure

Pakistan's insurance market is generally organized into the following segments, and SECP's statistics typically report them separately rather than as one blended "insurance" total:

- **Life Insurance** — insurers writing individual and group life policies. The life segment in Pakistan includes both conventional life insurers and, historically, a dominant state-linked player (State Life Insurance Corporation of Pakistan) alongside private life insurers.
- **Non-Life / General Insurance** — insurers writing property, motor, marine, fire, health, and other general (non-life) lines. This is typically the segment with the largest number of individual licensed companies.
- **Takaful Operators** — Sharia-compliant insurance providers operating on a mutual/cooperative risk-sharing model (as opposed to the conventional risk-transfer model), split in the same way as conventional insurance into:
  - **Family Takaful** (the takaful analogue of life insurance)
  - **General Takaful** (the takaful analogue of non-life/general insurance)
  Some conventional insurers also operate a separate "Window Takaful" operation (a segregated takaful window within a conventional insurer) alongside full standalone takaful operators — SECP statistics may distinguish window takaful operations from full-fledged takaful companies.
- **Reinsurance** — Pakistan Reinsurance Company Limited (PRCL) is the principal domestic reinsurer; insurers also cede risk to foreign reinsurers. Reinsurance figures (premium ceded, retention ratios) are typically reported distinctly from direct/primary insurance premium.

## Common Statistics Categories

When extracting figures from SECP's insurance-sector statistics, the following categories recur and should not be conflated:

- **Gross Written Premium (GWP)** — total premium underwritten by an insurer before any deduction for reinsurance ceded. This is the headline "top line" figure most often quoted for market size and ranking.
- **Net Premium / Net Premium Revenue** — premium retained by the insurer after ceding a portion to reinsurers; materially smaller than gross premium for insurers that cede heavily (e.g., large property/fire risks).
- **Net Earned Premium** — net premium adjusted for the unexpired portion of policy terms (an accounting-period concept distinct from both gross written and net premium).
- **Claims Ratio / Loss Ratio** — claims incurred (or paid) as a proportion of premium (commonly net premium or net earned premium, depending on the exact ratio definition used) — a core underwriting-performance metric reported by segment and sometimes by individual insurer.
- **Combined Ratio** — where reported, adds expense ratio to claims/loss ratio to give an overall underwriting profitability measure (below 100% generally indicating underwriting profit before investment income).
- **Total Assets / Investments** — balance-sheet size metrics used alongside premium volume to rank and compare insurers.
- **Takaful vs. Conventional Split** — statistics distinguishing takaful contributions (the takaful term for "premium," often called a "contribution" rather than a premium in Sharia-compliant terminology) from conventional premium, and tracking takaful's growing share of the overall market over time.
- **Market Share / Concentration** — derived figures (e.g., top-5 insurer share of GWP) calculated from insurer-wise premium or asset data rather than published as a primary line item.

## Terminology Note: "Premium" vs "Contribution"

In takaful, the amount a participant pays is technically a "contribution" (paid into a shared risk pool, often structured as a Wakala or Mudaraba model) rather than a "premium" in the conventional insurance sense, since takaful is structured as mutual risk-sharing rather than risk transfer to a shareholder-owned insurer. SECP statistics and takaful operators' own disclosures may use "contribution" and "premium" somewhat interchangeably in informal summaries, but a precise extraction should preserve which term the source document actually uses, especially when the question is about takaful specifically.

## Publication Cadence and Title

SECP publishes insurance-sector statistics on a periodic basis (this skill's own SKILL.md describes it as an "annual Insurance Industry Statistics publication"). The exact current title, publication frequency (annual vs. also-quarterly), and hosting location on `secp.gov.pk` should be verified directly against SECP's Insurance Division / Publications section at the time of use — SECP has reorganized its website and publication naming before, and this reference file does not have independently verified certainty on the exact current title, most recent edition year, or whether interim (e.g., quarterly or half-yearly) statistical releases also exist alongside the annual one. Treat any specific edition year or exact document title cited in an answer as something that should be confirmed against the live SECP source rather than assumed from this reference.

## Related But Distinct Regulatory Touchpoints

- Insurance sector systemic-risk commentary also appears in the State Bank of Pakistan's Financial Stability Review (see `sbp-financial-stability-review`), but that source does not provide the granular insurer-wise premium/asset statistics SECP itself publishes.
- SECP also regulates non-banking financial companies (NBFCs) and mutual funds, but those are reported through separate statistical series (see `mufap-mutual-funds` for mutual funds) and should not be blended with insurance-sector figures.
