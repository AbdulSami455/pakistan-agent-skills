## Debt Dynamics Identity (Standard IMF DSA Framework)

The general public-debt-dynamics decomposition used across IMF DSAs (and applicable to Pakistan using its own reported inputs) expresses the change in the debt-to-GDP ratio between two periods as the sum of:

1. **Interest-rate/growth-rate differential term**: the prior period's debt ratio multiplied by the gap between the effective (nominal) interest rate on debt and the nominal GDP growth rate. When the interest rate exceeds growth, this term pushes the debt ratio up even with a balanced primary budget ("r > g" dynamics); when growth exceeds the interest rate, this term pulls the ratio down.
2. **Primary balance term**: the primary deficit (revenue minus non-interest expenditure, as a negative contributor if in deficit) directly adds to or subtracts from the debt ratio.
3. **Other flows / stock-flow adjustment term**: includes exchange-rate valuation effects on foreign-currency-denominated debt (a PKR depreciation raises the PKR-GDP value of FX debt without any new borrowing), privatization proceeds, below-the-line financing operations, and other one-off adjustments.

The **debt-stabilizing primary balance** is the primary balance (as % of GDP) that exactly offsets the interest-rate/growth-rate differential term, holding the debt ratio constant. It rises when the interest-rate/growth differential is more adverse (higher rates relative to growth) and when the existing debt stock is larger. This is a standard macro-fiscal identity, not a Pakistan-specific formula — apply it using Pakistan's actual reported effective interest rate, nominal growth rate, and debt ratio rather than illustrative or memorized values.

## MAC DSA Framework as Applied to Pakistan

- Pakistan is assessed under the IMF's **Market Access Countries (MAC) DSA** framework (rather than the Low-Income Country Debt Sustainability Framework, LIC-DSF), reflecting its access to international capital markets (e.g., Eurobond issuance history).
- The MAC DSA framework uses risk-benchmark reference points for public debt-to-GDP and gross financing needs (GFN)-to-GDP to flag cases warranting deeper scrutiny — these are heuristic risk-signaling benchmarks embedded in the IMF's DSA template, not statutory or hard sustainability cutoffs, and the exact benchmark calibration should be confirmed against the specific DSA annex/vintage in use, since IMF DSA methodology is reviewed and can be recalibrated over time.
- Pakistan's own Finance Division/Debt Policy Coordination Office produces a periodic domestic Debt Sustainability Report projecting debt trajectories under baseline and stress/sensitivity scenarios (e.g., the FY2025-FY2027 report cited above, informed by the federal primary balance and Medium-Term Budget Strategy Paper projections). This is a complementary domestic publication, distinct from — and not necessarily using identical assumptions to — the IMF's own DSA annex published alongside Article IV or program review staff reports.

## Gross Financing Needs and Rollover Risk

- GFN (as % of GDP) = amortization (principal) falling due in the year + the primary deficit + interest due, roughly speaking; it captures near-term liquidity/rollover pressure distinct from the debt-stock ratio.
- A country can carry a moderate debt-stock ratio yet face elevated GFN/rollover risk if debt maturities are concentrated or external market access tightens — Pakistan's debt-sustainability discussions in IMF program contexts have specifically flagged financing-needs and rollover risk alongside the debt-stock trajectory, given reliance on short-maturity domestic instruments and bilateral/multilateral external financing.

## Extraction Notes

- Always separate the three components (interest-growth differential, primary balance, other flows/FX valuation) when explaining an observed change in Pakistan's debt ratio; do not attribute the full change to fiscal policy alone.
- Cite MAC DSA benchmark numbers only from a specific, current IMF DSA annex, and flag them as risk-signal thresholds, not absolute sustainability lines.
- Distinguish the IMF's DSA annex (program/Article IV specific) from Pakistan's own Finance Division Debt Sustainability Report (domestic, forward-projection publication) when citing either.
