## Recurring Publication Series/Formats

PIDE publishes across several recurring formats rather than a single flagship annual document. Do not assume any one of these is "the" PIDE report — a research question could be addressed in any of them, or in more than one over time.

- **PIDE Working Papers** — the main outlet for longer-form empirical or theoretical research by PIDE-affiliated economists (staff, visiting fellows, PhD researchers). Each has its own title, author(s), abstract, methodology, and dataset; numbered/sequenced but not chapter-linked to each other.
- **PIDE Knowledge Briefs** — short, accessible policy notes (often a few pages) that distill a research finding or policy argument for a non-technical audience. Frequently tied to a live policy debate (e.g., a budget measure, a regulatory proposal).
- **PIDE Discussion Papers** — working-paper-style output, sometimes used for more exploratory or preliminary analysis than a full Working Paper; treat findings here as provisional unless the paper states otherwise.
- **Policy Viewpoints** — opinion/analysis pieces by PIDE researchers or leadership arguing for a specific policy position; these are argumentative/persuasive in nature and should be clearly labeled as the author's viewpoint, not a neutral empirical finding.
- ***Pakistan Development Review* (PDR)** — PIDE's long-running, peer-reviewed academic economics journal (one of Pakistan's oldest economics journals). PDR articles go through academic peer review, which is a meaningfully higher evidentiary bar than an internal working paper or a policy viewpoint; when a claim is sourced to PDR versus a working paper, keep that distinction visible.
- PIDE also periodically produces **conference proceedings, books/monographs, and event-based reports** (e.g., from its annual General Meeting/AGM conference); these exist but are less commonly the target of a specific research-extraction question. ## Illustrative Research Themes (Describe Generally — Do Not Assume Exact Titles)

PIDE's research agenda spans macro and micro development economics topics. The following are well-known *themes* PIDE has published on repeatedly — useful for orienting a topic search — but specific paper titles, authors, and years must be looked up on PIDE's own site rather than assumed or fabricated:

- **Ease of doing business / regulatory reform** — PIDE has a recurring line of work critiquing regulatory burden, red tape, and the cost of doing business in Pakistan, often packaged as a "reform agenda" style publication or knowledge brief series aimed at deregulation.
- **Urban policy and the informal economy** — research on urbanization patterns, housing, informal settlements, and the size/behavior of the informal (undocumented) economy and its interaction with taxation and regulation.
- **Agriculture policy** — analysis of crop pricing/support-price policy, water management, land productivity, and agricultural market distortions.
- **Trade and industrial policy** — tariff structure, export competitiveness, and effective rate of protection studies.
- **Energy sector** — circular debt, energy pricing/subsidy analysis, and power-sector reform.
- **Tax policy and public finance** — tax base, FBR performance, and fiscal federalism (including NFC Award-related analysis).
- **Labor markets and education/human capital** — employment, skills mismatch, and returns to education.
- PIDE is also known more broadly for a "reform agenda"/institutional-reform framing across many of these topics — i.e., papers that diagnose a market or governance failure and then propose a specific policy fix — which is a recognizable house style but not a fixed template every paper follows.

Treat all of the above as **topic orientation only**. Do not present a specific paper title, exact publication year, or named author under any of these themes unless it has been confirmed directly from PIDE's website or a citation the user has provided.

## How to Search/Discover the Right Paper

Because PIDE has no single canonical document (unlike the PES's one-survey-per-year structure), finding the right paper requires an active search rather than a fixed chapter map:

1. **Search by keyword/topic on PIDE's own site** (`https://www.pide.org.pk`) — most think-tank sites expose a search bar and/or filterable publication list by category (Working Paper, Knowledge Brief, Discussion Paper, etc.) and by year.
2. **Search by author** if the user names a specific PIDE economist — PIDE researcher profile pages typically list their publications.
3. **Search by category/series first, then narrow by keyword** — e.g., filter to "Knowledge Briefs" if the user wants a short policy take, or "Working Papers" if they want full methodology.
4. **Use general web search** (`site:pide.org.pk <keyword>`) as a fallback when the site's own search/filter tools don't surface a match, since think-tank sites' internal search can be limited.
5. **Check *Pakistan Development Review* separately** — PDR articles may not appear in the same listing as working papers/knowledge briefs, since it is run more like an academic journal (potentially with its own archive/issue structure by volume and year).
6. **When a title, author, or year cannot be confirmed**, say so explicitly rather than presenting a best guess as fact — PIDE's catalog is large and titles are easy to misremember or conflate across similar papers on the same topic.

## Extraction Notes

- Never fabricate a specific PIDE paper title, author name, or exact publication year — these must come from the actual source document or a live site lookup. If uncertain, say the paper needs to be located/verified on PIDE's site rather than presenting a guessed title as fact.
- Keep the publication series (Working Paper vs. Knowledge Brief vs. Discussion Paper vs. PDR article vs. Policy Viewpoint) attached to any citation, since the evidentiary weight and intended audience differ meaningfully across formats — a PDR article has passed peer review; a Policy Viewpoint has not and is explicitly argumentative.
- Cross-reference `pes-*` or `sbp-*` skills for official statistics that a PIDE paper might cite or critique — do not let a PIDE estimate silently substitute for an official figure.
