# Pakistan Commercial Arbitration

## Overview

Use this skill for practitioner-level questions on arbitration law in Pakistan: which statute governs a given arbitration (domestic vs. foreign-seated/foreign award enforcement), how a foreign arbitral award is recognized and enforced in a Pakistani court, the well-documented weaknesses of the 1940 Act and the reform effort aimed at replacing it, and Pakistan's track record as a respondent state in investment-treaty (ICSID) arbitration. This is a dispute-resolution-mechanism skill, not a general court-litigation or judicial-statistics skill.

## Coverage

- **Domestic arbitration — Arbitration Act, 1940**: a pre-independence, colonial-era statute that remains the governing law for domestic (Pakistan-seated, Pakistan-law-governed) arbitrations. It does not distinguish domestic from international arbitrations in its drafting and is widely criticized by practitioners for permitting extensive court intervention (e.g., at the reference-to-arbitration stage, during the arbitral process, and at the award-challenge stage), which undercuts arbitration's efficiency advantage over litigation. It provides for three broad categories of arbitration: without court intervention, arbitration where no suit is pending but conducted through court process, and arbitration in a pending suit through court.
- **Foreign awards / New York Convention — Recognition and Enforcement (Arbitration Agreements and Foreign Arbitral Awards) Act, 2011 (REA 2011)**: Pakistan acceded to the New York Convention (signed 1958, ratified 2005) and implemented it domestically first via a 2005 Ordinance (repeatedly re-promulgated) and finally via the REA 2011, an Act of Parliament. REA 2011 gives the High Courts jurisdiction to recognize and enforce foreign arbitral awards (and to refer parties to arbitration under a valid arbitration agreement) largely as if the award were an order of the High Court, with refusal grounds limited to those in Article V of the New York Convention (e.g., invalid arbitration agreement, due-process defects, award beyond scope of submission, tribunal composition/procedure not per agreement, award not yet binding or set aside at the seat, non-arbitrability, or public-policy conflict). Do not treat REA 2011 as replacing the 1940 Act for domestic arbitrations — the two statutes operate in separate lanes (foreign awards/agreements vs. domestic arbitration).
- **Pending reform — Draft Arbitration Bill/Act 2024**: a draft Arbitration Act, modeled substantially on the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration, has been circulated and discussed (including presentation to the Federal Law Minister in 2024) as a proposed replacement for the 1940 Act, aimed at modernizing domestic arbitration and limiting judicial intervention. As of the most recent verifiable reporting, this remains a draft/bill that has not been enacted into law — treat any claim that "Pakistan adopted a new Arbitration Act in 2024" as unverified/likely premature unless independently confirmed against Parliament's own record or the Gazette, and route to `parliament-legislative-tracker` for the bill's live status.
- **Investment-treaty arbitration (ICSID)**: Pakistan has been a respondent in significant investment-treaty arbitrations, most prominently Tethyan Copper Company Pty Limited v. Islamic Republic of Pakistan (ICSID Case No. ARB/12/1), arising from the Reko Diq mining-lease dispute in Balochistan under the Australia-Pakistan bilateral investment treaty; the tribunal found Pakistan liable for breaches including fair-and-equitable-treatment and unlawful expropriation, with a very large (multi-billion-dollar) damages award later the subject of settlement negotiations between the parties. Treat any specific dollar figure, settlement term, or case-status update as needing verification against current reporting, since this dispute has evolved over years including post-award settlement discussions.
- **Institutional and ad hoc practice**: Pakistani commercial arbitration remains predominantly ad hoc rather than institutional; awareness and use of institutional rules (ICC, LCIA, SIAC, or a domestic institution) exists but is not the default mode, and courts' historical willingness to intervene in the arbitral process has been cited by practitioners as a factor limiting confidence in arbitration as a genuinely faster alternative to litigation in Pakistan-seated matters.

## Use This Skill For

- identifying which statute governs a given arbitration: Arbitration Act 1940 (domestic), REA 2011 (foreign awards/agreements and New York Convention enforcement), or (once enacted) a modernized Arbitration Act
- explaining the process and limited grounds for refusing recognition/enforcement of a foreign arbitral award in a Pakistani High Court under REA 2011
- explaining the well-documented structural weaknesses of the 1940 Act (extensive court intervention at multiple stages) that motivate the reform effort
- correctly stating the current status of the Draft Arbitration Bill/Act 2024 (not yet enacted, as of the most recent verified reporting) rather than assuming it is in force
- summarizing Pakistan's ICSID/investment-treaty arbitration exposure, especially the Reko Diq/Tethyan Copper dispute, at a general level while flagging specific figures/status as needing current verification
- distinguishing a commercial arbitration clause dispute (governed by REA 2011/Arbitration Act 1940) from an investment-treaty arbitration (governed by the relevant BIT and ICSID Convention/UNCITRAL rules) — these operate under entirely different legal regimes even though both are "arbitration involving Pakistan"

## When Not to Use This Skill

- For the live legislative status of the Draft Arbitration Bill 2024 as it moves through Parliament — use `parliament-legislative-tracker` for the bill's current stage; use this skill for the substantive/structural content of what the bill proposes to change.
- For a FIDIC construction-contract arbitration clause specifically (as opposed to arbitration law generally) — use `fidic-construction-contracts-pakistan` for the construction-contract-specific framing, though the underlying arbitration-law mechanics described here still apply.

## Routing Rules

- If the question is about a domestic, Pakistan-seated arbitration under Pakistani law, apply the Arbitration Act 1940 framework and flag its court-intervention-heavy character.
- If the question is about enforcing a foreign-seated award or a foreign arbitration agreement in Pakistan, apply the REA 2011/New York Convention framework and note the High Court's jurisdiction and the limited Article V-style refusal grounds.
- If the question asks about "Pakistan's new arbitration law" or similar, confirm whether the user means the still-pending Draft Arbitration Bill/Act 2024 (not yet in force, verify current status) before answering as if a new regime were already operative.
- If the question is about an investment/state-investor dispute (e.g., a foreign investor suing Pakistan under a BIT) rather than a private commercial arbitration between two contracting parties, route to the ICSID/investment-treaty-arbitration framing, not the REA 2011/1940 Act commercial framework — different legal regime, different forum, different remedies.
- If the question is about a bill's live legislative progress, route to `parliament-legislative-tracker`.
- If the question is about a FIDIC-based construction dispute specifically, cross-reference `fidic-construction-contracts-pakistan`.

## Extraction Workflow

1. Determine whether the arbitration in question is domestic (Pakistan-seated, between private commercial parties) or involves recognition/enforcement of a foreign award/agreement — this determines whether the 1940 Act or REA 2011 governs.
2. If domestic, identify which of the three 1940-Act arbitration categories applies (without court intervention / no suit pending but through court / in a pending suit through court), since the court's role differs materially across these.
3. If foreign-award enforcement, identify which High Court has jurisdiction and confirm whether a refusal-of-enforcement argument is being raised — if so, map it to one of the limited New York Convention Article V-style grounds rather than treating enforcement as freely resistible on the merits.
4. If the question references a "new" or "2024" Arbitration Act, verify current enactment status before answering — as of the most recent verified reporting this is a draft/bill, not yet in force; do not assert it has replaced the 1940 Act without independent confirmation.
5. If the question concerns an investment-treaty dispute (state as respondent), identify the specific case (e.g., Tethyan Copper/Reko Diq) and treat any damages figure, settlement term, or current case status as needing verification against current reporting rather than a fixed historical figure.
6. Note whether institutional rules (ICC, LCIA, SIAC, etc.) or ad hoc arbitration governs the specific matter, since this affects procedural mechanics beyond the base statute.
7. Flag that this skill provides structural/procedural orientation only and recommend a licensed arbitration/dispute-resolution lawyer for drafting, enforcement, or challenge of a specific arbitration clause or award.

## Technical Rules

- The Arbitration Act 1940 and REA 2011 are not alternatives for the same dispute — the 1940 Act governs domestic arbitration procedure; REA 2011 governs recognition/enforcement of foreign arbitration agreements and foreign arbitral awards under the New York Convention. Do not apply 1940-Act court-intervention mechanics to a foreign-award enforcement question governed by REA 2011.
- REA 2011 confines a Pakistani court's refusal-of-enforcement grounds to the New York Convention's limited categories (Article V-equivalent) — do not treat foreign-award enforcement in Pakistan as open to full merits review by the enforcing court.
- The Draft Arbitration Bill/Act 2024 is, as of the most recently verified reporting, not yet enacted — treat any claim that it is currently in force as requiring fresh verification, since legislative status can change and this is exactly the kind of claim prone to going stale.
- Investment-treaty (ICSID) arbitration against Pakistan as a respondent state is a fundamentally different legal regime from private commercial arbitration between two contracting parties — it arises from a bilateral/multilateral investment treaty and is heard under ICSID Convention or UNCITRAL rules, not under the 1940 Act or REA 2011; do not conflate the two when discussing "arbitration in Pakistan."
- Do not state a specific ICSID award figure, settlement amount, or case-status detail as current fact without flagging it as needing verification against current reporting, given that high-value investment disputes often evolve through post-award negotiation, annulment proceedings, or settlement over an extended period.
