# PPRA Public Procurement Reference

## Source

- Public Procurement Regulatory Authority (PPRA) official website: `https://www.ppra.org.pk` (verify current domain before citing as a live link).
- Governing law: Public Procurement Regulatory Authority Ordinance, 2002.
- Governing rules: Public Procurement Rules, 2004 (as amended by subsequent SROs/notifications).
- PPRA's website hosts the federal e-procurement/tender-notice system where procuring agencies publish tender advertisements.

## What PPRA Is and Is Not

- PPRA is a **federal regulatory and oversight body**. It writes and administers procurement rules for federal government procuring agencies (ministries, divisions, attached departments, and federal public-sector organizations).
- PPRA is **not** a purchasing/spending agency — it does not buy goods, award contracts, or disburse public funds itself. Each procuring agency conducts and is accountable for its own procurement, within PPRA's rules.
- PPRA does not itself perform audits of completed spending — post-facto financial audit is the domain of the Auditor-General of Pakistan (AGP), a separate constitutional office.

## Procurement Methods (Federal PPRA Rules, 2004)

| Method | When Used |
|---|---|
| **Open Competitive Bidding** | The default/preferred method for all procurement above minimum value thresholds; requires public advertisement (PPRA website, and historically newspapers) and a transparent bid-evaluation process. |
| **Limited Tendering** | Permitted only in specified circumstances (e.g., a limited number of known suppliers, time constraints, or value below a lower threshold) — not a routine substitute for open bidding. |
| **Direct Contracting / Single-Source Procurement** | Permitted only where justified (e.g., proprietary technology, sole supplier, emergency, or a genuinely unique requirement) and typically requires specific written justification/approval. |
| **Request for Quotations (RFQ)** | Used for low-value procurements below a minimum threshold, typically requiring at least a small number of competing quotations. |

(Exact Rupee thresholds separating these tiers are set in the Rules/subsequent SROs and are revised periodically — verify current values on PPRA's website rather than citing a fixed number from memory, since this reference intentionally avoids presenting a potentially stale threshold as settled fact.)

## Advertisement / Publication Requirements

- Procurements above the notified threshold generally require advertisement on PPRA's website/e-procurement portal; higher-value procurements have historically also required publication in national newspapers (in addition to the PPRA website) to ensure wide reach.
- PPRA has moved toward electronic procurement processing over time (an e-procurement/e-Pak Acquisition and Disposal-type system) — verify the current platform name, URL, and whether e-procurement is mandatory or optional for a given procurement type, as this has evolved and is best confirmed on PPRA's current site rather than assumed.
- Tender notices typically include: scope of procurement, eligibility criteria, bid security requirements, submission deadline, and bid-opening date/venue.

## Blacklisting

- PPRA Rules provide for blacklisting a supplier/contractor found to have committed a procurement-rules violation — e.g., submission of false statements/documents, collusive bidding, non-performance, or corrupt/fraudulent practice in the procurement process.
- Blacklisting is typically initiated by the procuring agency (sometimes with PPRA's involvement/confirmation) and results in the firm being barred from participating in government procurement for a specified period.
- Blacklisting is a procurement-eligibility sanction — it is legally and administratively distinct from:
  - a company's SECP registration/incorporation status (see `secp-company-registry`),
  - a criminal conviction (which would run through the ordinary courts or, for corruption-specific cases, NAB/Accountability Courts — see `nab-accountability-data`),
  - a CCP antitrust finding for bid-rigging/collusion under the Competition Act (see `ccp-competition-enforcement`).
- A blacklisted firm can, in principle, still be simultaneously the subject of a CCP cartel investigation if the same conduct (e.g., bid-rigging on government tenders) also violates the Competition Act's prohibited-agreements provisions — these are parallel, independent proceedings under different laws.

## Federal vs. Provincial Procurement Authorities

Pakistan does **not** have a single unified procurement regulator. PPRA (federal) rules apply only to federal procuring agencies. Each province and Gilgit-Baltistan has enacted its own, separately administered procurement law, rules, and portal:

| Jurisdiction | Authority | Approx. Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Federal | Public Procurement Regulatory Authority (PPRA) | PPRA Ordinance, 2002 + PPRA Rules, 2004 |
| Punjab | Punjab Procurement Regulatory Authority (PPRA-Punjab) | Punjab Procurement Regulatory Authority Act, 2009 (verify) |
| Sindh | Sindh Public Procurement Regulatory Authority (SPPRA) | Sindh Public Procurement Act, 2009 (verify) |
| Khyber Pakhtunkhwa | Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Public Procurement Regulatory Authority (KPPRA) | KP Public Procurement Regulatory Authority Act (verify year) |
| Balochistan | Balochistan Public Procurement Regulatory Authority (BPPRA) | Balochistan Public Procurement Act (verify year) |
| Gilgit-Baltistan | GB procurement regime | (verify current legal basis/authority name) |

Each provincial authority publishes its own tender notices on its own portal and sets its own thresholds and procedures, which may differ from federal PPRA's. Do not assume federal PPRA rules or thresholds apply to a provincial or district-government tender — always confirm which jurisdiction's authority actually governs the specific procuring entity in question, and verify current legal-basis years/citations against each authority's own website since exact enactment years are easy to misstate from memory.

## Extraction Notes

- Always identify whether the procuring entity is federal or provincial before citing a procurement rule or threshold.
- State the procurement method actually used, and check it against the value-based tiering in the Rules before calling it compliant or non-compliant.
- For blacklisting questions, always specify: which procuring agency/authority took the action, the stated grounds, the effective duration, and current status (active bar, expired, under legal challenge).
- Do not conflate PPRA's procurement-rules oversight with the Auditor-General's post-facto financial audit function, or with CCP's antitrust enforcement, or with NAB's corruption-prosecution function — each is a distinct institution with a distinct mandate, even though all four can, in some cases, touch the same underlying government contract.
