# UNHCR Pakistan Refugee Data Reference

## Registration Categories

Afghan nationals in Pakistan are tracked under several distinct registration/documentation categories. These categories are not interchangeable, and any population figure must specify which category (or combination) it refers to:

- **Proof of Registration (PoR) card holders** — Afghans registered under the joint Government of Pakistan / UNHCR registration exercises (the main such exercise took place in 2006-2007, with the PoR card scheme periodically extended since). PoR cardholders are formally recognized as registered Afghan refugees in Pakistan and PoR validity has been extended multiple times via government notifications (often for short periods at a time, sometimes with gaps), rather than existing as a single permanent status — the current PoR validity/expiry date should always be checked against the latest government/UNHCR announcement rather than assumed.
- **Afghan Citizen Card (ACC) holders** — a separate documentation category introduced in 2017 through a joint registration exercise (led by Pakistan's National Database and Registration Authority, NADRA, with UNHCR/IOM/government support) for undocumented Afghans who did not hold a PoR card. The ACC is a distinct legal category from the PoR card — it does not confer refugee status in the same formal sense and has historically had its own separate validity extensions and separate policy treatment (e.g., ACC holders have at times faced different rules than PoR holders regarding registration/repatriation deadlines).
- **Undocumented Afghans** — Afghans present in Pakistan without either a PoR card or an ACC. UNHCR and the Government of Pakistan have published periodic estimates of this population, but by definition these are estimates rather than a registered count, and estimates have varied significantly across sources and time — always flag an undocumented-population figure as an estimate, not a registration count.

Do not sum PoR and ACC figures into a single "refugee" total without checking whether the source has already done so — UNHCR's own reporting sometimes presents these as separate line items specifically to preserve the distinction, since only PoR holders are treated as registered refugees under the arrangement UNHCR operates.

## "Persons of Concern" Categories

UNHCR's global data model classifies "persons of concern" into several standard categories, and Pakistan reporting should be read against this framework:

- **Refugees** — persons who meet the refugee definition (under the 1951 Refugee Convention/1967 Protocol framework, or, in Pakistan's case, under the bilateral/tripartite arrangements since Pakistan is not a signatory to the 1951 Convention) and are recognized as such. In the Pakistan context, PoR cardholders are the population UNHCR and the government have treated as registered refugees on a prima facie basis, even though Pakistan's domestic legal framework does not have a standing national refugee law.
- **Asylum-seekers** — individuals who have applied for refugee status (typically through UNHCR's mandate refugee status determination process in Pakistan for non-Afghan nationalities, since Afghans have generally been handled through the registration/PoR mechanism rather than individual RSD) but whose claim has not yet been decided. Pakistan hosts a smaller population of non-Afghan asylum-seekers and mandate refugees (e.g., from other countries) processed through UNHCR's individual RSD procedures — these are a numerically much smaller and distinct population from the Afghan PoR/ACC caseload and should not be merged with Afghan refugee figures.
- **Others of concern** — a residual UNHCR category that can include stateless persons, returnees being monitored, or other groups depending on context; check the specific dataset's category definitions rather than assuming a fixed universal list applies to every UNHCR Pakistan release.

Because Pakistan is host to one of the largest and longest-running Afghan displacement situations globally, the overwhelming majority of "persons of concern" figures for Pakistan refer to the Afghan PoR/ACC caseload rather than the smaller non-Afghan asylum-seeker/mandate refugee caseload — but the two should still be reported separately when a source distinguishes them.

## The 2023-2025 "Illegal Foreigners' Repatriation Plan" — A Major Discontinuity

**This is essential context for interpreting any Afghan population time series that spans this period.** In October 2023, the Government of Pakistan announced the "Illegal Foreigners' Repatriation Plan," directing all foreign nationals without valid documentation to leave Pakistan or face arrest and deportation, with an initial deadline of 1 November 2023. This plan:

- Was initially targeted at undocumented Afghans, but its implementation and subsequent phases also affected ACC holders and, at later stages, created pressure and uncertainty even for PoR cardholders, as Pakistan issued further deadlines and phased repatriation directives through 2024 and into 2025 (reported in phases sometimes described in media and UN reporting as targeting ACC holders in a "second phase" and PoR holders in later phases).
- Triggered a large, well-documented wave of returns/deportations of Afghans from Pakistan to Afghanistan, with UNHCR, IOM, and OCHA situation reports recording several hundred thousand to over a million Afghan departures/returns across late 2023 through 2024-2025 (exact cumulative totals vary by source, reporting date, and whether "returns" includes only IOM/UNHCR-assisted movement or also self-directed departures — do not cite a single precise cumulative total without checking the specific, dated situation report it comes from).
- Caused a **sharp, real drop in registered Afghan population figures** in Pakistan (PoR and ACC counts) across this period, driven by actual departures, card cancellations/non-renewals, and shifting registration-category treatment — not by a data or methodology artifact alone, though methodology/category changes (e.g., how ACC holders were counted as the policy phases evolved) compound the discontinuity.
- Means that **any time series comparing Afghan refugee/registered population figures in Pakistan from before October 2023 against figures from 2024 or later will show a large decline that reflects a genuine, policy-driven demographic and political event, not routine year-over-year fluctuation.** This should always be flagged explicitly when presenting or comparing such a time series, rather than presented as a smooth trend line.

### Practical Extraction Guidance

- When a question spans 2023 into 2024/2025, explicitly note the repatriation plan as the driver of any large population decline rather than silently reporting the raw numbers.
- Confirm whether a cited figure is from before or after the plan's announcement (October 2023) — this single date is the key inflection point.
- Be cautious with any specific cumulative "returnee" figure — these accumulate over time and change with each new situation report; state the report's publication date alongside any number.
- Recognize that repatriation figures (an outflow/movement count, tracked by IOM/UNHCR return-monitoring mechanisms) are conceptually different from a change in the *registered* population count (a stock figure derived from PoR/ACC registries) — a returnee count and a registered-population decline are related but not arithmetically identical, since registry updates, card cancellations, and counting-methodology changes also move the registered stock independently of confirmed physical returns.

## Verification Note

Specific cumulative return figures, exact repatriation-plan phase dates/deadlines, and current PoR/ACC validity extension dates change frequently and have been updated multiple times since October 2023. Treat any such figure or date in an answer as needing confirmation against the most recent UNHCR Pakistan Operational Data Portal release or OCHA/IOM situation report rather than assumed fixed facts.
