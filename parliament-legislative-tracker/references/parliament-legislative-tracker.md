# Parliament Legislative Tracker Reference

## The Legislative Process (Federal)

Pakistan's federal legislative process runs through the bicameral Majlis-e-Shoora (Parliament): the National Assembly (NA, the lower house, directly elected) and the Senate (the upper house, representing the provinces equally plus other units). The stages below are the general path for an ordinary bill; money bills follow a distinct, NA-dominant path (see below).

1. **Introduction (First Reading)**: A bill is introduced ("moved for leave to introduce") in either house — except money bills, which under Article 73 can only originate in the National Assembly. The mover may be a federal minister (a "Government Bill") or an ordinary member (a "Private Member's Bill"). On introduction, the bill is usually referred to the relevant Standing Committee.
2. **Committee Referral and Report**: The relevant Standing Committee (e.g., Standing Committee on Interior, on Law and Justice, on Finance, etc., depending on subject matter) examines the bill, may hear stakeholders, and reports back with or without amendments. Committee stage is where most substantive changes to bill text happen.
3. **Second Reading**: The house considers the bill clause-by-clause along with any committee amendments; further amendments can be moved from the floor at this stage.
4. **Third Reading and Passage**: The house votes to pass the bill as a whole (typically after the clause-by-clause stage is complete). A simple majority of those present and voting is generally sufficient for ordinary legislation; constitutional amendments require a two-thirds majority in each house (Article 239).
5. **Transmission to the Other House**: A bill passed by one house is transmitted to the other house, which may pass it as-is, amend it, or reject it.
6. **Resolving Disagreement Between Houses**:
   - For an ordinary (non-money) bill: if the houses disagree — one rejects it, proposes amendments the other doesn't accept, or 90 days pass without action — either house's presiding officer may summon a **joint sitting** of both houses (Article 70/71 mechanism), where the bill is considered by members of both chambers voting together, and passage requires a majority of members present and voting in the joint sitting.
   - For a **money bill (Finance Bill)**: under Article 73, a money bill originates only in the NA; once passed by the NA, it goes to the Senate, but the Senate cannot reject or amend it beyond making recommendations within a set period — the NA is not bound to accept the Senate's recommendations, reflecting the NA's constitutional primacy over money bills.
7. **Presidential Assent**: A bill passed by both houses (or by a joint sitting, where applicable) is presented to the President, who must assent within the constitutionally specified period; if the President neither assents nor returns it within that period, it is deemed to have been assented to. If the President returns a non-money bill with observations, the houses (in joint sitting, if needed) can re-pass it, after which assent becomes mandatory. Upon assent, the bill is gazetted and becomes an **Act of Parliament**.

## Bill vs. Act vs. Ordinance

These three terms are not interchangeable, and mixing them up is a common extraction error:

- **Bill**: a draft piece of legislation under consideration by Parliament. It has no legal force. A bill remains a bill through introduction, committee stage, and both readings — right up until presidential assent.
- **Act**: the same text once it has passed both houses (or a joint sitting) and received presidential assent, and has been published in the official Gazette of Pakistan. Only an Act has the force of law as ordinary legislation. Track the transition from "Bill" to "Act" specifically at the assent/gazette-notification step — this is the one confirming event that a bill has actually become law.
- **Ordinance**: a law promulgated directly by the **President** (or, for provincial subjects, a provincial Governor) under Article 89 of the Constitution, used when the National Assembly is not in session and the President is satisfied that circumstances require immediate action. Key characteristics:
  - An Ordinance has the same force and effect as an Act of Parliament for as long as it remains in effect, but it is not itself an Act — it bypasses the normal bill-reading process at the time of promulgation.
  - It has a **limited validity period**: under Article 89 (as amended), an Ordinance must be laid before the National Assembly (and Senate, if it deals with a matter that would fall to both houses) and stands repealed at the expiry of a constitutionally fixed period unless extended or unless both houses pass a resolution disapproving it earlier. The exact number of days for the initial period and for any extension has been amended more than once historically (via constitutional amendment) — do not assert a specific day-count as current fact without checking Article 89's text as currently in force; state the mechanism (fixed initial period, extendable once, then lapses without further extension) rather than a specific figure if uncertain.
  - Because an Ordinance lapses automatically without ratification, a common pattern in Pakistani legislative history is chained/re-promulgated Ordinances used to keep a measure in effect while a bill version works through Parliament, or in place of one entirely. When tracking a piece of federal legislation, always check whether it began life as an Ordinance and whether it was later formally enacted as a Bill/Act, or allowed to lapse, or re-promulgated.

**Extraction rule of thumb**: If a source describes something as "promulgated," it is almost certainly an Ordinance, not a Bill or Act. If it describes something as "passed by the National Assembly/Senate" and later "assented to," it is following the Bill-to-Act path.

## Federal-Only Scope

This skill tracks legislation in the **National Assembly and Senate only** — Pakistan's federal legislature. It does not cover:

- Provincial assembly legislation (Punjab Assembly, Sindh Assembly, KP Assembly, Balochistan Assembly), which follows an analogous but institutionally separate process in each province, tracked (where available) on that province's own assembly website — out of scope for this skill.
- AJK Legislative Assembly or Gilgit-Baltistan Assembly legislation — these territories have their own legislatures and legislative tracking is out of scope here as well.
- Subject-matter allocation between federal and provincial legislatures follows the Constitution's Federal Legislative List (post-18th Amendment, most residual subjects devolved to the provinces) — if a bill's subject matter is plausibly provincial, verify it is actually a federal bill (i.e., on the Federal Legislative List or a concurrent-type federal matter) before treating it as in-scope for this skill.

## Extraction Notes

- Standing Committee names and jurisdictions can shift between assemblies/sessions; confirm the committee name against the current NA/Senate committee list rather than assuming continuity across assemblies.
- "Passed by NA" is not equivalent to "became law" — always check for Senate concurrence (or joint sitting outcome) and presidential assent before describing a bill as enacted.
- A bill can lapse at the end of a National Assembly's term (dissolution) if not passed; a new Assembly generally has to have the bill reintroduced. Note this when a tracked bill spans an election/dissolution.
- Ordinances are numbered and dated at promulgation and published in the Gazette of Pakistan just like Acts — check the publication type (Ordinance vs. Act) in the gazette notification itself if the source is ambiguous.
